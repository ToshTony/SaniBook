Introduction: Why Sanitation Matters

Sanitation is a critical foundation for public health, environmental sustainability, and economic development. Beyond toilets and pipes, sanitation protects dignity and unlocks economic value. The SaniBook makes the case that addressing sanitation is one of Kenya's highest-return investments.

A national imperative

Millions lack safely managed sanitation — fueling disease, lost productivity, and environmental harm. The SaniBook reframes the problem as an opportunity: a bankable pipeline of high-impact projects that will create jobs, improve health outcomes and accelerate Kenya's Vision 2030.

The Unseen Current

No neighbourhood is an island. The invisible bridge of contamination — vectors, water, air and economic strain — connects every community. SaniBook explains how poor sanitation in one area affects the whole region and why coordinated action is essential.

  • Vectors don't discriminate: flies and mosquitoes spread disease across boundaries.
  • Water knows no boundaries: runoff and groundwater link communities.
  • Economic ripple: outbreaks strain hospitals and livelihoods across regions.

The Sanitation Challenge: Deep Dive

A short primer explaining how sanitation impacts communities beyond immediate health — property values, environment, stigma, and shared resilience.

1. The Unseen Current

Poor sanitation does not stay confined. Through vectors, water, and air, sanitation issues cross boundaries and threaten entire regions.

1.1 Vectors & contamination

Disease-carrying flies and other vectors easily move between communities, transporting pathogens from open waste to food and water.

1.2 Water & shared resources

Runoff and seepage contaminate groundwater and rivers, undermining water safety across regions.

1.3 Ripple effects

From property values to tourism and investor confidence, sanitation failures create wide economic and social costs.
